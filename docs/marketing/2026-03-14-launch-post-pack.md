### Post 1 — What This Is (Week 1, Day 1)

There are thousands of AI tools.

Directories list them. Newsletters rank them. YouTube videos compare them.

We built TrustMeBroAI to answer that one question.

### Post 2 — The One-Answer Decision (Week 1, Day 5)
*Theme: Core differentiator. Invites conversation.*

---

Most AI tool products give you more options, not fewer.

Top 10 lists. Comparison tables. Side-by-side scores.

We made the opposite decision.

TrustMeBroAI gives you one answer. One tool. One reason. Then a button to go try it.

The reasoning: if you already knew which tool to use, you would not be asking. The job is to make the decision for you — not to hand the decision back in a fancier format.

This means we have to be confident enough in the recommendation to stake the product's reputation on it.

So far, that feels like the right call.

What would you want from a tool like this — a confident single answer, or more options to choose from?

### Post 3 — The Business Model (Week 2, Day 8)
*Theme: Honest about the email ask.*

---

TrustMeBroAI is free.

Here is how that actually works.

You answer three questions. We give you a recommendation. To see which tool we picked, you enter your email.

That is the whole deal.

No subscription. No ads. No VC money telling us which tools to recommend.

The email is how we keep the lights on — eventually through an email list that is worth something to the people on it, and to us.

We put that in the consent copy on the product:

*"I agree to receive product updates and AI tool recommendations by email. (It's also how we keep this free — no VC money, just your inbox.)"*

Some people will find that too blunt. We think it is better than pretending the email has no value.

[link to trustmebro.ai]

### Post 4 — Behind the Locked Card (Week 2, Day 11)
*Theme: Product decision. Behind the build.*

---

The result page has a locked card.

You complete the wizard, see two tools that did not make the cut for you, and then there is a blurred card with a lock icon. Enter your email to see what is behind it.

We recently redesigned that card.

The old version was a generic blur — grey placeholder rectangles and a padlock. It communicated "something is hidden here" but not what or why it was worth unlocking.

The new version shows a realistic blurred preview of what is behind it: a logo, a name, a match percentage. You can tell it is a specific recommendation, not a generic result.

The copy under the lock changed too: *"Your #1 match is waiting — enter your email to unlock."*

The point: the gate should feel like something specific is being revealed, not like a generic paywall.
